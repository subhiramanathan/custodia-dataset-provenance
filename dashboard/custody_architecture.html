◆ Custodia — chain of custody for training data

Problem Statement

Proving a model trained only on dataset X is worthless if X was never trustworthy.

Intent: "Trustworthy" isn't one property — it's three separate questions, each checkable on its own: was the source honest, was the handling honest, can a stranger verify the result without taking my word for it.

What's novel hereI took a pattern built to catch an AI agent quietly overstepping its authority across a chain of hand-offs — and rebuilt it, from scratch, to catch a data pipeline lying about what it did to your records. That cross-domain transfer is the idea; the cryptography underneath is standard, decades-old machinery, not the point. Then, live, I found a gap in my own transfer and closed it — I'd been checking what a step claimed, never who was allowed to claim it.

Limitations & Future Work
Limitations

Every layer here proves custody — the data wasn't altered in transit, and someone is accountable for it. That's a real guarantee, and it's also a boundary I drew on purpose: no layer inspects content, only the chain of custody around it. A curator can honestly sign data that was poisoned before it ever reached them — this system says nothing about that, by design, not by oversight.

Where I'd take this

That boundary is exactly where the real research question starts — closing it needs multi-party attestation or content-level verification. I'm not claiming to have solved it. I'm claiming to have found precisely where it begins, and that's where I'd want to spend a fellowship.
